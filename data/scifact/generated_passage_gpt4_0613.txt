Instead, it is a systemic process that is determined early in development by the entire organism rather than at the individual cellular level. This means that while in some organisms, each individual somatic cell contains the information necessary to determine its own sex, in galliform birds, this is not the case. Passeriformes, a large order of birds also known as perching birds or songbirds, exhibit a unique method of sex determination within their somatic cells. This process is referred to as cell autonomous sex determination. Unlike in mammals, where sex is determined by the presence or absence of the Y chromosome, in passeriformes, it is the different combination of Z and W chromosomes that determine sex. Males have two Z chromosomes (ZZ), while females have a Z and a W chromosome (ZW).   The cell autonomous sex determination occurs when each cell 'decides' its sex independently, based on its chromosome composition. This means that in passeriformes, the ZW cells become female, and ZZ cells become male, irrespective of the sex of the organism. This phenomenon allows for the presence of gynandromorphs, birds that are male on one side and female on the other.  This is a stark contrast to the system in mammals, where all cells in a male body are male and all cells in a female body are female. In passeriformes, the sex of the individual bird is determined by the majority of its cells, but individual cells can be of a different sex than the organism they belong to. This demonstrates the remarkable complexity and diversity of biological sex determination processes in the animal kingdom.

De Novo assembly of sequence data indeed generates more specific contigs than unassembled sequence data. On the other hand, De Novo assembly is a process that aligns and merges these fragments without any reference sequence, creating a set of overlapping reads that represent regions of a genome. This process generates contigs that are more specific, as they provide a more organized and interconnected representation of the original sequence. This is particularly beneficial in the study of non-model organisms, where reference genomes are not available. Therefore, De Novo assembly of sequence data has more specific contigs, providing a more detailed and accurate picture of the genetic information.
